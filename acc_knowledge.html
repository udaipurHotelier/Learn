Basic Accounting Terms

Assets: Resources owned by a business that are expected to bring future economic benefits (e.g., cash, buildings, equipment).

Liabilities: Obligations or debts that a business owes to others (e.g., loans, accounts payable).

Equity: The owner's claim on the assets of the business after liabilities are paid off. It's also called "Owner's Equity" or "Shareholders' Equity".

Revenue: The income generated from normal business activities (e.g., sales, service income).

Expenses: The costs incurred in the process of earning revenue (e.g., salaries, rent, utilities).

Profit and Loss: The difference between revenue and expenses. A business makes a profit if revenue exceeds expenses, and incurs a loss if expenses exceed revenue.

Basic Accounting Equation

The fundamental accounting equation is:
Assets = Liabilities + Equity
This equation shows that everything a company owns (assets) is either financed by borrowing (liabilities) or by the owners (equity).

Types of Accounts

Current Assets: Cash or assets expected to be converted into cash within a year (e.g., cash, accounts receivable, inventory).
Non-Current Assets: Assets that are not expected to be converted into cash within a year (e.g., property, plant, equipment).

Current Liabilities: Obligations due within one year (e.g., accounts payable, short-term loans).
Non-Current Liabilities: Obligations due after one year (e.g., long-term debt, bonds payable).

Accounting Principles
Some key accounting principles include:
  1. Accrual Principle: Revenues and expenses are recognized when they are incurred, not when cash is received or paid.
  2. Consistency Principle: Once an accounting method is chosen, it should be used consistently from period to period.
  3. Going Concern Principle: Assumes that a business will continue to operate indefinitely.
  4. Matching Principle: Expenses should be recorded in the period in which the related revenue is earned.
Double-Entry Bookkeeping

  1. Debit (Dr): Increases assets or expenses, and decreases liabilities or equity.
  2. Credit (Cr): Increases liabilities or equity, and decreases assets or expenses.
    For example, if a business buys inventory for cash, the transaction would be:
  1. Debit Inventory (increase in asset)
  2. Credit Cash (decrease in asset)
The Financial Statements

Balance Sheet: Shows the financial position of a company at a specific point in time. It provides a snapshot of assets, liabilities, and equity.
Formula: Assets = Liabilities + Equity

Income Statement (Profit and Loss Statement): Shows the companyâ€™s performance over a period of time. It details the companyâ€™s revenues, expenses, and profits or losses.
Formula: Revenue - Expenses = Profit (or Loss)

Cash Flow Statement: Reports the cash inflows and outflows during a period, categorized into operating, investing, and financing activities.
It helps understand how changes in balance sheet accounts and income affect cash and cash equivalents.

Statement of Changes in Equity: Shows the movement in equity over a period, detailing items such as retained earnings, stock issued, and dividends paid.

Journal Entries and Ledger
  1. Journal: A chronological record of all transactions. Each entry typically includes the date, accounts affected, amounts, and a brief description.
    Example of a journal entry:
    Date: 01/01/2024
    Account: Cash (Dr), Sales Revenue (Cr)
    Amount: $500

  2. Ledger: A record of all accounts and their balances. All journal entries are posted into the respective ledger accounts for easier tracking of balances.
Trial Balance

A trial balance is a report that lists the balances of all general ledger accounts. It ensures that the sum of debits equals the sum of credits, which helps to verify the accuracy of the ledger entries.

Depreciation

Depreciation is the process of allocating the cost of a tangible fixed asset over its useful life. This is done to match the cost of the asset with the revenue it generates over time.

  1. Straight-line depreciation is the most common method, where an equal amount of depreciation is charged each year.

Taxation

A basic understanding of tax concepts is also important:

  1. GST (Goods and Services Tax): A value-added tax levied on the sale of goods and services in countries like India.
  2. Income Tax: Tax levied on individual or corporate income.
  3. Tax Returns: Periodic filings that report the taxable income and the tax payable by an individual or company.
Career Path in Accounting

As a fresher, you may begin as an Account Assistant, Accounts Executive, or Junior Accountant. Over time, with experience and further qualifications (like CPA, CMA, or ACCA), you can move to roles such as

  1. Senior Accountant
  2. Accounting Manager
  3. Financial Analyst
  4. Tax Consultant
  5. Auditor
